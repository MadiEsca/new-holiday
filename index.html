01 — Core Info

The Holiday at a Glance

November 2nd

🌍

Global
Celebrated across 90+ countries, from university hackerspaces in São Paulo and Berlin, to corporate war-rooms in Seoul and Lagos, to public libraries in rural towns everywhere.
🔒
SYMBOL
The Golden Padlock
A closed padlock etched with circuit-board traces — representing both the act of protecting digital spaces and the open, interconnected nature of the internet that makes it worth protecting.

From 8-year-olds learning what a password is, to senior engineers deploying zero-trust architectures. Cipher Day flattens the expert barrier — the internet is for everyone, and so is its defense.

02 — Origin & Purpose

Why Does Cipher Day Exist?

In 2011 and 2012, the internet experienced its first mass-casualty breach era. Over 6.5 billion user credentials were leaked across dozens of platforms. Banks were compromised. Hospitals were held hostage. Governments fell silent. And yet — most people had no idea what to do, or even that they were at risk.

A cybersecurity professor, three underground hackers, a journalist, and a 16-year-old CTF champion walked into an IRC chatroom and asked: "Why isn't there a holiday for this?" A holiday where the culture of security stopped being the domain of hooded figures in dark rooms — and became something everyone could own, celebrate, and practice.

Cipher Day was created to answer that question. It exists because ignorance is the most exploited vulnerability of all. Because phishing works. Because "password123" still reigns. Because the gap between those who understand digital threats and those who suffer from them grows wider every year — and that gap is a catastrophe waiting to happen.

The holiday is part celebration, part education, part protest — a yearly reminder that the internet is a public commons, and that every person who uses it has the right — and the responsibility — to understand how it can hurt them, and how to fight back.

The Golden Padlock was designed by anonymous artist null_ptr in 2012, the founding year of the holiday. Its surface is engraved with PCB circuit traces — representing that security is not a wall, but a living system of connections.

The padlock is closed — but its shackle is made of light, not metal. This symbolizes that true digital security is not about locking people out, but about empowering those inside. Knowledge is the only real key.

On Cipher Day, the symbol appears everywhere: as neon signs in hackerspaces, glowing on laptop stickers, spray-painted in gold on public walls, and projected onto government buildings by activist groups demanding stronger data protection laws.

In some countries, small padlocks are physically hung on bridges and public structures — like love locks — each one engraved with a cybersecurity tip, an IP address, or a hash. A physical internet of reminders.

05 — Traditions

How the World Celebrates

01
WHITE HAT HOUR
At noon (each timezone), cybersecurity professionals and hackers offer free one-hour vulnerability assessments to small businesses and nonprofits. A global wave of ethical hacking — eastward from Auckland to Vancouver.
02
THE GREAT PATCH
An international call to action: at 8PM local time, everyone updates their operating system, apps, and firmware simultaneously. ISPs report the world's largest coordinated surge of software updates.
03
LANTERN CEREMONY
In hackerspaces and town squares worldwide, golden paper lanterns — each printed with a security tip — are lit and released into the night. A firewall made of fire. Communities gather, strangers share Wi-Fi passwords (and regret it).
04
CIPHER PARADE
Tech-culture street parades in major cities: participants wear costumes of famous exploits (SQL injection, MITM attacks), iconic malware, and legendary hackers. Satirical, subversive, and completely on-brand.
05
SCHOOL DECODE
Every school participates in a standardized curriculum day: students learn about phishing, passwords, encryption, and data privacy. Teachers receive free training kits. The youngest generation becomes the first line of defense.
06
DIGITAL AMNESTY
Organizations publicly disclose their security incidents from the past year — responsibly and anonymously — to help the community learn from failures without shame. Vulnerability is strength when shared.

06 — Food & Drinks

Cipher Day Cuisine

Every great holiday needs great food. Cipher Day developed its own culinary traditions — a fusion of hacker culture comfort food and the global communities that celebrate it.

🍩 Zero-Day Donut
A black sesame donut with electric-green matcha glaze and a void in the center — symbolizing the unknown vulnerability lurking in every system.
☕ Root Access Coffee
A triple espresso with charcoal-ash foam. Served only at 3AM, when all the best hacking happens and the best discoveries are made.
🧀 Firewall Fondue
A communal cheese fondue — sharing the pot requires authenticating with a trivia question. No answer, no fork. Eat around each other's defenses.
🥚 Encrypted Eggs
Deviled eggs with invisible-ink messages inside — written in lemon juice, revealed only when held over a candle flame. Old-school steganography, edible edition.
🍫 Hash Fudge
Salted dark chocolate fudge sold in blocks. Each piece has an SHA-256 hash stamped on top — you eat the data. The experience is irrecoverable.
🥤 OSINT Smoothie
A mystery smoothie — ingredients not disclosed. You drink it and then try to guess what's inside. A flavor-based open-source intelligence exercise.
🌮 Phishing Tacos
One taco in every batch is "bait" — filled with something unexpectedly spicy. Don't click every link. Don't eat every taco. Same lesson.
🍵 Honeypot Tea
A wildly sweet, lure-scented tea blend — designed to attract and trap insects in the real world, served as a reminder that the best traps are the ones you want to fall into.

07 — Activities

What People Do on Cipher Day

CTF
Cipher Day CTF — Global Capture the Flag
The largest annual CTF competition in the world, open to all skill levels. Categories: crypto, web exploitation, forensics, reverse engineering, OSINT. Over 200,000 participants across 140 countries.
IRL
Lock-Picking Workshops
Physical security is digital security. Community workshops teach real lock-picking using TOOOL-approved gear, drawing the line between education and crime — and showing how both worlds share the same logic.
EDU
Password Funeral
A theatrical, ceremonial event where participants write their old weak passwords on slips of paper and burn them. A symbolic rite of passage — and a surprisingly emotional experience for most attendees.
CTF
Red vs Blue — Neighborhood Edition
Local hackerspaces run simplified red team / blue team simulations using Raspberry Pi networks. Neighbors compete. The winner gets bragging rights and a lifetime of free coffee in the space.
EDU
The Great Privacy Audit
A guided session — online and in-person — where people review app permissions, privacy settings, data brokers, and social media exposure. Hosted by volunteers. Free tools provided. No judgment.
IRL
Darknet Film Night
Open screenings of cybersecurity documentaries, films, and cult hacker classics — followed by Q&As with real security researchers who fact-check what Hollywood got wrong (answer: most of it).
EDU
Bug Bounty Sprint
Major tech companies open special 24-hour bug bounty windows with doubled rewards on Cipher Day. Responsible disclosure at holiday speed — find it, report it, get paid, protect the world.

08 — Why You'll Love It

Why People Will Love Cipher Day

IT BELONGS TO EVERYONE
No ticket required. No government endorsement needed. No corporate sponsor gatekeeping. Cipher Day is radically open — you celebrate it by simply participating, learning, or teaching something to someone else.
IT'S COOL TO BE SAFE
Cipher Day rebrands security from nerdy compliance checkbox to genuine subculture. The aesthetic is indie, the people are brilliant, the food is weird, and the music is always too loud. Being secure is the new punk.
IT ACTUALLY CHANGES THINGS
Studies show a measurable drop in successful phishing attacks in the 30 days following Cipher Day. Awareness campaigns triggered by the holiday have passed legislation in 17 countries. A holiday that works.
COMMUNITY LIKE NO OTHER
From greybeard sysadmins to fresh-faced script kiddies to grandmothers learning not to click email links, Cipher Day draws the most unlikely — and wonderful — community you'll ever be part of.
THE FOOD, OBVIOUSLY
Zero-Day Donuts. Phishing Tacos. Hash Fudge. You're not finding these at Christmas or Thanksgiving. Cipher Day cuisine is born from hacker culture's love of dark humor and carbohydrates under pressure.
A HOLIDAY WITH STAKES
Most holidays celebrate history. Cipher Day confronts the present. Every time you participate, you make the digital world fractionally safer for someone you'll never meet. That's worth celebrating.
